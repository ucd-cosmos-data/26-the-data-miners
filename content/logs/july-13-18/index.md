    text: "The day began with an introduction to supervised learning and how labeled examples allow models to learn a relationship between input features and a target. We compared regression, which predicts a continuous value, with classification, which predicts a category, and discussed the bias-variance tradeoff between underfitting and overfitting. The prediction lecture introduced methods ranging from linear, polynomial, ridge, and lasso regression to decision trees, random forests, boosting, and stacking. During lab, we presented our first mini projects and began using scikit-learn's consistent fit-and-predict workflow. A data-splitting activity showed why random splitting may be inappropriate when observations are ordered by time, location, group, or individual. We finished with mathematical notation for inputs, targets, model parameters, predictions, and loss functions."
  - name: "Tuesday"
    date: "July 14"
    datetime: "2026-07-14"
    text: "Our second mini project began in lab with the California Housing dataset. After reviewing linear regression and how coefficients are chosen to minimize squared residuals, we used k-means clustering to create location-informed housing market groups using geographic coordinates and median home value. We then prepared a report explaining the features, preprocessing choices, cluster assignments, and limitations of interpreting the resulting regions. The day's lecture shifted from regression to classification, introducing decision trees that recursively split features to create purer groups, as well as ensemble methods such as bagging, boosting, and random forests. We also studied unsupervised methods including k-means, hierarchical and spectral clustering, PCA, and t-SNE, along with distance and similarity measures used when labels are unavailable."
  - name: "Wednesday"
    date: "July 15"
    datetime: "2026-07-15"
    text: "Model validation was the main focus of the morning. We examined split-sample validation and cross-validation, where different subsets are held out so that performance is measured on observations the model did not train on. We connected these procedures to the bias-variance tradeoff and compared metrics such as mean squared error, root mean squared error, and R-squared for regression; classification metrics; and cluster-quality measures for unsupervised learning. We also discussed how information criteria balance fit against model complexity. Later, distinguished lecturer David Gagnon spoke about the science behind baseball, showing how biomechanics, computer vision, and statistical models contribute to the analysis of player performance. Our group received the Siena Scalp EEG dataset for the final project and began considering its signal channels, repeated measurements, metadata, and possible prediction or classification questions. A Q&A with David Gagnon brought the day to a close."
